Reporting

Where all the work comes together: a clear, honest, and actionable description of what you found, what it means, and what to do next.

Why reporting is critical

A penetration test that is not followed by a good report has limited value. Organizations need to understand which issues exist, how severe they are, and how to fix them. A strong report helps engineering teams prioritize work and gives leadership a realistic view of risk.

For ethical hackers, reporting is also a way to demonstrate professionalism and build trust.

Typical report structure

While formats differ, InfoSecKe‚Äôs templates include a few common parts. There is usually an executive summary, a section describing scope and methodology, a prioritized list of findings, and detailed technical appendices with evidence.

Labs encourage you to practice writing concise but complete entries for each finding, rather than just collecting screenshots.

Writing clear findings

Each finding in a report should answer specific questions: what the issue is, where it was observed, how it can be reproduced, what the impact is, and how it might be remediated. It should also have a clear severity rating and enough context that a reader understands why it matters.

In labs, you practice this by writing small reports for solved challenges and comparing them with community writeups.

Writing for multiple audiences

A good report can be read by both technical and non-technical stakeholders. That is why many practitioners include a high-level summary in simple language, followed by more detailed sections for engineers and security teams.

InfoSecKe encourages you to think about who will read your reports in the real world and to tailor tone and detail accordingly.

Ethics in reporting

Reporting is also part of your ethical responsibility. You avoid exaggerating impact, you clearly note any limitations in testing, and you handle sensitive data carefully in screenshots and logs.

When applicable, you also align with responsible disclosure practices so that vulnerabilities are fixed before any public discussion.

Practice writing reports

Use InfoSecKe templates and your lab results to build a portfolio of reports. This will help you in job interviews, bug bounty programs, and real-world engagements.
