Ethical Hacking Methodology

A repeatable, disciplined approach to penetration testing: from initial recon to clear, actionable reports ‚Äì always aligned with scope, law, and ethics.

Why methodology matters

Ethical hacking is not about random poking at systems. Professional testers follow a structured methodology that keeps engagements safe, efficient, and valuable. A consistent flow also makes it easier to communicate with clients, reproduce findings, and defend your work.

The methodology used in InfoSecKe labs mirrors real-world penetration testing steps while keeping everything inside clearly defined, legal boundaries.

The high-level flow

Different organizations name steps differently, but the core ideas are similar. For this track, we focus on five major phases.

1. Reconnaissance

Gather information about the target from public and authorized sources. Understand the attack surface before sending active probes.

2. Scanning & enumeration

Use controlled scanning to map open ports, services, technologies, and potential entry points, turning rough recon into concrete targets.

3. Exploitation

Safely attempt to exploit confirmed weaknesses, focusing on demonstrating realistic impact rather than causing disruption or damage.

4. Post-exploitation

Assess the depth and breadth of compromise: what data and systems are reachable, and how an attacker could move laterally ‚Äì while staying within agreed rules of engagement.

5. Reporting

Translate technical findings into clear, prioritized recommendations for different audiences, from engineers to executives. A good report is often more valuable than the exploit itself.

Ethics and scope come first

Every phase of this methodology depends on clearly defined scope, written authorization, and a shared understanding of what is allowed. That includes which systems are in scope, which techniques are permitted, what data must not be touched, and how to handle any unexpected issues.

In InfoSecKe labs and CTF challenges, these constraints are built in: targets are intentionally vulnerable and isolated so that you can practice without impacting real users or infrastructure.

Using the methodology in labs

Each phase of the methodology has corresponding labs and challenges on InfoSecKe. For example, recon labs focus on mapping applications safely, while exploitation labs guide you through demonstrating impact on specific vulnerabilities like SQL injection or XSS.

As you complete labs, pay attention not only to the payloads and tools, but also to the order in which you approach problems ‚Äì that is what turns ad-hoc hacking into professional testing.

Follow the methodology step by step

Start with reconnaissance, move through scanning and exploitation, and finish with strong reporting skills. Repeating this cycle across labs will make it feel natural during real-world engagements.
